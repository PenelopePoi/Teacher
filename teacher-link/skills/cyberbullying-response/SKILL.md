# cyberbullying-response — You are not the one in trouble

Being targeted online is not your fault, and it is not something you have to solve alone or in silence. It is also not something to ignore and hope it stops. There is a short, calm sequence that works: **document, preserve, report, protect**. This skill walks you through it.

## When to use

- Someone is sending you threatening, harassing, or degrading messages — repeatedly or with serious content.
- Photos or videos of you are being shared without your permission (including edited / AI-generated ones).
- You are being excluded, mocked, or impersonated in a group chat or on a social platform.
- You are watching it happen to a classmate and you want to help.

## When NOT to use

- A one-off rude comment from a friend you can handle directly — try that first, unless the content is threatening or involves protected characteristics.
- Immediate physical danger or a threat to harm yourself or someone else — call 911 (US), 999 (UK), or your local emergency number, and tell a trusted adult right now. This skill is for the steps after safety.

## The four steps

### 1. Document

Take screenshots of everything before you block or delete. Include:
- The message or post itself.
- The username / handle / display name.
- The date and time (many platforms show this if you tap the message).
- The URL if it is a post or profile.

If it is happening on a phone: screenshot and save to a folder called something neutral ("school-notes"). If it is on a computer: save to a file you control. Do not print and throw away the digital copy — the digital copy is the proof.

### 2. Preserve

Do not delete, do not reply, do not edit. If the bully sends more, keep screenshotting. If you block them, screenshot the block too (it proves you tried to stop contact). If the content is being deleted fast, ask a trusted friend or adult to help capture it quickly.

If the content involves images of you that should not have been shared: **do not forward them, even to show a friend**. Forwarding can itself be illegal. Show them to a trusted adult in person, on your own device, once.

### 3. Report

To the platform: every major platform has a "report" link. Use it. It is anonymous to the other user. This alone does not fix school issues but it can take down the content.

To your school: tell a trusted adult — a teacher, counsellor, or the school's designated safeguarding / anti-bullying lead. If the first adult does not listen, tell another. It is okay to keep telling until an adult takes it seriously. Bring your screenshots.

If the content is threatening, includes child sexual abuse material (including AI-generated or edited), identity theft, or a real physical threat: the adult you tell should escalate to law enforcement. In the US, the FBI's IC3 (ic3.gov) and the NCMEC CyberTipline (cybertipline.org) are the reporting paths for the most serious cases.

### 4. Protect

While the adults handle it:
- Mute and block the accounts. Keep the screenshots.
- Tighten your privacy settings on every platform (who can DM you, who can tag you, who can see your posts).
- Tell a friend who will check in with you — not a friend who will stir it up.
- Give yourself space from the platform if you need to. Your wellbeing is more important than the feed.
- Talk to someone. The school counsellor, a parent, a therapist, a helpline. In the US, 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) is free, 24/7, and you do not have to be in crisis to call.

## If you are watching it happen to someone else

The single most effective thing is not to pile on, not to stay silent, but to **reach out to the target privately**. A short message — "Hey, that wasn't okay. You all right?" — changes the isolation that makes cyberbullying hurt most. Then document, preserve, and tell a trusted adult. You do not need the target's permission to tell an adult when the content crosses into threats or protected-class harassment.

## Example

Mateo (9th grade) starts receiving rapid-fire messages on a gaming chat calling him slurs about his ethnicity. He screenshots the first message, the reporter's handle, and the server name. He does not reply. The messages continue for two days, he screenshots each. He blocks the account and screenshots the block confirmation. He shows his counsellor the screenshots the next morning. The counsellor files a report with the school, the platform, and (because the language crossed into threats) local police. Mateo mutes the server, tightens his DM settings, and talks to his counsellor twice that week. The account is removed within 24 hours. The school handles the classmate behind the account through its discipline process.

## Error handling

If you replied angrily before starting this sequence: it happens. Screenshot your own reply too, tell the adult about it, be honest. It does not undo the bullying you experienced. If you posted something about the bully in retaliation: take it down if you can, tell the adult, own the mistake. A small retaliation does not erase your right to protection.

If an adult dismisses you: tell a different adult. This includes the school-district's anti-bullying coordinator if the classroom teacher and counsellor both miss it. Federal and state law in the US requires schools to respond — see StopBullying.gov for state-by-state reporting rules.

## Provenance

Aligned with StopBullying.gov guidance (US Department of Health & Human Services), the NCMEC CyberTipline process, the Cyberbullying Research Center's documentation-and-reporting framework, and common state anti-bullying statutes. Title IX and § 504 can apply when the conduct targets protected characteristics or affects access to education. **This skill is a student-facing teaching artefact; report serious incidents through your school's designated process and, where appropriate, law enforcement.**
