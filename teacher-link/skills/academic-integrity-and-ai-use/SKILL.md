# academic-integrity-and-ai-use — Your brain, with a co-pilot

AI tools are part of how you learn now. The integrity question is not "did you use AI?" — it is "are you honest about how, and is the work you turned in your thinking?" A grade is a signal about what you can do. Hiding AI use breaks that signal, for you and for every classmate who did it on their own.

## When to use

- You used an AI tool anywhere in the process (brainstorming, outlining, drafting, editing, debugging, translating).
- Your teacher's policy is unclear and you need a default that is defensible.
- You are citing sources and the AI surfaced some of them.
- You are unsure whether you crossed the line from "helped me think" to "did the work for me."

## When NOT to use

- The class is a closed exam or a timed in-class essay where no outside help is allowed — follow the exam rules, no AI.
- You are a teacher designing policy — this skill teaches students; policy design is for the educator track.

## The three-question self-check

Before you turn it in, answer these three out loud:

1. **Can I defend every claim and every paragraph without the AI?** If not, you are turning in the AI's work with your name on it.
2. **Did the AI generate the core idea, or did it refine my idea?** Generating ≠ refining. Both can be okay; only one needs a disclosure.
3. **If the teacher asked me to rewrite one paragraph in 10 minutes by hand, could I?** If no, you don't understand what you handed in.

## Disclosure — what to write

If your teacher's policy requires disclosure, include a short note at the end of the assignment. Plain language. Specifics matter more than the format.

> **AI use note.** I used ChatGPT (GPT-5) to brainstorm three possible thesis angles and to suggest counter-arguments for paragraph 3. I wrote the draft and revisions myself. The quotes from *The Great Gatsby* are from the book, not the model. Prompt on file if requested.

Four things in that note: **which tool**, **what for**, **what you wrote yourself**, **source of any facts or quotes**.

## Citation — when the AI surfaced a source

If the AI pointed you to a real source (a book, a paper, an article), cite the source itself, not the AI. Verify the source exists — AI tools still fabricate citations. Open it, read it, cite it from what you read.

If the AI wrote text you quote verbatim, cite the tool. The 2025 MLA and APA provisional guidance is: name the tool, the version, the date, and (if possible) include the prompt and the output in an appendix.

## Workflow

1. **Read the assignment twice.** Note any rule about AI use. If it says "no AI," stop here and do it without.
2. **Decide your AI use in advance.** "I'll use it for brainstorming but write the draft myself" is a plan. "I'll see where it takes me" is how you end up in trouble.
3. **Keep the prompts and outputs.** A short doc alongside your draft. If you are ever asked, you can show your process.
4. **Write with the AI off, at least for one full paragraph.** This is your proof that you can.
5. **Use the three-question self-check** before turning it in.
6. **Add the disclosure note** if your teacher's policy requires it, or if you are unsure.
7. **Cite the sources the AI surfaced**, not the AI — after you open and read them.

## Example

8th-grade ELA. Assignment: five-paragraph essay on symbolism in *Animal Farm*. Policy: "AI allowed for brainstorming and feedback; not for drafting."

Jaya uses Claude to ask "what are three recurring symbols in *Animal Farm* that relate to power?" She gets: the windmill, the barn, the farmhouse. She picks the windmill, writes her own outline, writes her own draft. She asks Claude to "give three tough questions a reader might ask about paragraph 3" and revises based on two of them. She does not ask Claude to rewrite any sentence.

Disclosure:

> I used Claude (Sonnet 4.6) to brainstorm symbols and to critique paragraph 3. I wrote the essay myself. Quotes are from the Signet Classics edition of *Animal Farm*.

If her teacher asks her to defend paragraph 4 in class, she can. That is the test.

## Error handling

If you realise halfway through that the AI wrote more than you intended: stop, save what you have, start the draft again in a new document without AI. Turning in the AI-heavy draft and hoping no one notices is how honest mistakes become integrity violations. Teachers understand mid-course correction. They do not understand cover-ups.

If a teacher accuses you and you did disclose honestly: your prompt log and your disclosure are the evidence. Calm, specific, show the work. If you did not disclose and should have: own it. The consequence of a late disclosure is smaller than the consequence of being caught hiding.
